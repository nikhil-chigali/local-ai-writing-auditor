Few dishes have traveled as far — geographically, culturally, and culinarily — as ramen. What began as a humble immigrant food on the docks of Yokohama has become one of the most beloved, obsessed-over, and endlessly remixed dishes on the planet. Here's everything you need to know.

Ramen is a Japanese adaptation of Chinese wheat noodle soups, first recorded in Yokohama's Chinatown in the early 20th century. Chinese workers selling meals from food carts likely introduced the dish to Japanese laborers at the ports of Yokohama, Kobe, and Nagasaki — it was cheap, filling, and fast. The dish was called shina soba ("Chinese noodle") well into the 1950s, a name that only gave way to "ramen" in the post-war era.

The first dedicated ramen shop on record was Rai Rai Ken, opened in Asakusa, Tokyo around 1910. It employed Chinese cooks and served a noodle dish topped with roasted pork, fish cake, and nori — a combination that would become the template for the modern bowl.
